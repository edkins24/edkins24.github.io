Exploring Census Data¶

As a particle physicist, almost all of my work has been done using C++ and ROOT (https://root.cern.ch/). ROOT is a very powerful tool for scientific analysis and can easily handle huge amounts of data, but is rather specific in its applications. After several years of working with data describing particle interactions, thinking in terms of "events" and "trees" (if you know, you know), I wanted to get a feel for working with different types of data, both in content and format. I was especially interested in what one would commonly encounter in publicly available data sets, taken from the web. Of course, ideally it would also be of interest to me, personally. I began by exploring the website for the United States Census Bureau (https://www.census.gov/). This turned out to be a tremendous resource, full of a wide range of statistics describing just about every aspect of the US population. One thing that stood out to me as I browsed the data for various regions was the marked difference in mean income for males and females. This difference also appeared when comparing the income of foreign born versus native populations. As a woman in a predominantly male field and the wife of an immigrant, this naturally caught my interest. A pattern that I have observed is that women in fields where they are under-represented often seek out female mentors. I, myself, have a female advisor. In fact, out of the 6 people in our small research group, 5 are female. In a similar fashion, immigrants often form communities of their own within the larger society to which they belong. The reasons for this are fairly obvious: working somewhere where you are the only female, or living in a city where hardly anyone looks like you, eats the food that you eat or speaks the language you grew up with is difficult, to say the least. Intuitively, it seems that people are better off when they have the benefit and comfort of those with similar backgrounds and experiences. But is this actually true? I wondered whether immigrants tend to fare better in places where there are many other immigrants, or if perhaps, instead, being unique lends some advantage. Simply put, is there truly strength in numbers, or is it better to stand out in a crowd?

The data¶

The data I used for this analysis are the 5-year estimates from the 2011-2015 American Community Survey. Since there are large, systematic differences between rural and urban communties, I restricted my search to urban areas only. Although it is possible to include information on racial and ethnic demographics, for this first look I compare characteristics of native and foreign born populations only, without regard to race, ethnicity, or citizenship status. The downloaded data comes in csv format, making it ideally suited to the pandas data analysis library in Python.

Fraction of population which are immigrants¶

I want to see how immigrants fare in urban areas as a function of their concentration. In other words, the fraction of an urban population which is foreign born. I, therefore, want to calculate the ratio of the foreign population to the total population and save this information for later. I also want to get an idea of how this ratio is distributed among the urban areas.

In [5]:
foreignNum = pd.to_numeric(df['HC03_EST_VC01'].iloc[1:]).astype(float)
totalNum = pd.to_numeric(df['HC01_EST_VC01'].iloc[1:]).astype(float)
foreignFrac = foreignNum / totalNum
plt.hist(foreignFrac, bins=50)
plt.xlabel('Fraction of population which is foreign born')
plt.ylabel('Number of Urban Areas')
Out[5]:

So the majority of cities have populations which are approximately between 5 and 15% foreign born. There are several cities with populations which are more than a quarter immigrants, including one outlier with a 50% immigrant fraction. Where is this?

In [6]:
df['GEO.display-label'].iloc[foreignFrac.idxmax()]
Out[6]:
'San Luis, AZ Urban Cluster (2010)'

A quick check reveals that the city of San Luis, AZ, located near the border with Mexico, has a population which is 98.9% Hispanic. It's possible that such an fringe data point may have characteristics which are very different from most cities, meaning that including it may bias the results. On the other hand, it likely represents the extreme of immigration, and as such should be considered in this analysis. I choose to retain it for now, although it would be worthwhile to compare the results after it is removed from the data set.

Out of curiosity, what city has the smallest immigrant population?

Population Characteristics¶

Next I need to decide which characteristics best indicate success (or lack thereof). The first and most obvious is simply mean income (in dollars). We can also examine the distribution of income brackets for native and foreign populations, as well as the incidence of poverty. Of course money is not the only measure of success. Arguably of equal importance is the level of education attained. Owning your own home or business isn't too shabby, either. But since income disparity is what inspired this work, I'll start there.

So I can see that income is similarly distributed for foreign born and native populations, with a tight clustering of incomes for regions with small immigrant populations and an increasingly larger spread as immigrant populations increase. However, for this first look, what I am really interested in is how incomes change with increasing immigrant fractions on average. For this, I want to group the immigrant fractions in bins, and look at the average income for each bin.

This is surprising. It seems that immigrants make less on average in cities where there are many immigrants than in cities where there are few. In fact, in cities where less than about 6% of the population is foreign born, immigrants tend to make a bit more than native born populations. What about other measures of success, such as education?

As the immigrant fraction increases, the number of foreign born with less than a high school degree markedly increases. Perhaps cities with a large immigrant fraction also tend to have a large number of jobs available which do not require a degree. If this is so, a similar pattern may hold for the native population.

The pattern for the native born population looks very different than for the immigrant population. In particular, the education levels are fairly flat for most cities, regardless of immigrant fraction. However, the fraction of the native population without a high school diploma does rise sharply in cities with more than 20% of the population comprised of immigrants. This suggests that these cities may, indeed, have more jobs available that do not require a degree. Additionally, the number of immigrants with graduate degrees is higher than the same for the native population in low immigrant fraction cities (near 20% versus about 10%). This suggests that immigrants with graduate or professional degrees likely came to these cities after getting their degree, likely in response to a job offer. This also may provide a hint as to why immigrants in cities with a low fraction of foreign born earn slightly more than the native population, as they tend to be highly skilled. Finally, given these results, it would be instructive to look at how the types of occupations are distributed amongst the native and foreign born populations.

Conclusion¶

Exploring this data was really interesting, but it is clear that this is just the tip of the iceberg - so many more questions appeared for me. For example, in order to examine the effects of immigrant concentration on measures of success, I looked at the average in a given population bin. For cities with immgrant fractions on the lower end, between about 5 and 15%, where most of the cities were grouped, the incomes of foreign born populations were fairly tightly clustered. However, as the immigrant fractions increased, the spread in incomes between cities became substantial. What is the difference between these cities? Why are some cities with large immigrant fractions seemingly so successful? Additionally, I note that there are many factors which can cause high immigrant fraction to be correlated to lower incomes and education level. Likely it is very difficult to come to a new city if you don't speak the language and don't have an education, so such immigrants may tend to only move to cities where they know people, or know that there will be many others who do speak in their native tongue. I also did not consider race here, which undoubtably has an influence as well. I plan on revisiting this data in greater detail in the future.

I think my take away is that, although we are all most confortable surrounded by people who look like us, we shouldn't be afraid to step out of our comfort zone. When you are the odd man (or woman) out, it may seem like all eyes are on you, but maybe this isn't such a bad thing, after all. :)
